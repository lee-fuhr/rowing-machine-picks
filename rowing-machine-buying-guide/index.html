How to choose the right rowing machine

Updated June 2026Independently researchedNo paid placement.

Quick answer

If you want a whisper-quiet, smooth rowing workout without the maintenance of water or air rowers, a magnetic rowing machine is your best bet. Focus on models with strong build quality, programmable resistance, and a comfortable seat, then check owner reviews for long-term reliability.

What makes a magnetic rower different from air or water rowers?

Magnetic rowing machines use a flywheel and a set of magnets to create resistance. Unlike air rowers where a fan spins faster as you pull harder, or water rowers where a paddle churns water, magnetic resistance stays consistent and close to silent. That means you can row early in the morning or late at night without waking anyone up. The trade-off is that the feel isn’t as dynamic as what you get from an air rower like the Concept2. The resistance curve is smoother, but experienced rowers sometimes say it lacks the “full-boat” sensation of water or the progressive drag of air. Still, for most home users, the quietness and low maintenance of magnetic resistance outweighs that difference.

How much should you spend on a magnetic rowing machine?

You’ll find magnetic rowers in three broad price bands. Budget-friendly machines often have manual magnetic resistance with a limited number of levels and basic LCD displays. They typically pack down for storage but might feel less sturdy under heavy use. Mid-range models usually add more resistance levels, better build materials, and some form of app connectivity or Bluetooth. Premium machines step up to electromagnetic resistance with automatic load changes, larger screens, and subscription-based training libraries. A good rule of thumb is to buy the best build quality you can afford. Read recent owner reviews to see if cheaper models develop wobble or noise after a few months. Spending in the middle or upper end of the market often means a smoother ride and better resale value down the road.

What features actually matter for a great rowing experience?

Start with the seat and rail. A well-padded seat that glides smoothly on a sturdy aluminum rail makes a huge difference in comfort during longer sessions. Check the rail length: if you’re tall (over six feet), you’ll want a rail that accommodates a full leg extension. Weight capacity is another overlooked spec, look for at least some mentioned limit that aligns with your needs. Next, consider resistance adjustability. Manual resistance with a knob is simple and reliable, while electromagnetic resistance lets you program interval workouts and connect to apps like Kinomap or Zwift. If you’re motivated by data, look for a machine that tracks stroke rate, distance, calories, and heart rate, either on its own display or via a companion app. Foldability or upright storage can be a big plus if you’re short on space.

Are magnetic rowing machines good for serious fitness?

Absolutely, provided you understand their strengths. Magnetic rowers excel at steady-state cardio, low-impact HIIT, and recovery days. The resistance is smooth and easy on the joints, and because you can fine-tune the load, they’re great for beginners and rehabilitation. Many high-end magnetic models simulate real rowing dynamics with computer-controlled drag, so you get a challenging workout at any intensity. That said, if your primary goal is to replicate competitive rowing or you need the instant feedback of an air rower’s resistance curve, a magnetic rower may feel a bit polite. Competitive rowers often prefer the air- or water-based machines for training specificity. But for general fitness, weight loss, and long, quiet sessions, a good magnetic rower is a fantastic tool.

How do you choose the right magnetic rower for your home?

First, measure your available floor space and note whether you’ll need to stow the machine after each workout. Some magnetic rowers fold vertically, others slide on wheels, and a few split into two pieces. Look at the footprint and storage method in relation to your room layout. Next, think about noise tolerance: if you exercise in a shared apartment or next to a sleeping baby, the near-silent operation of a magnetic rower is a major advantage. Also consider your tech appetite. If you love structured classes and on-screen coaching, a model with a large touchscreen and subscription platform might be worth the extra cost. If you prefer simple manual workouts or your own streamed content, a basic display with Bluetooth heart rate pairing is plenty. Finally, read what owners say about customer support, especially warranty length and parts availability, before committing.

Frequently asked questions

Can magnetic rowers simulate the feel of water or air resistance?

Most magnetic rowers offer a smooth, constant resistance that doesn’t perfectly mimic the dynamic feel of water or air. However, higher-end electromagnetic models can vary the load programmatically to simulate changing drag, so they come closer than basic manual magnetic units.

Are magnetic rowing machines quieter than other types?

Yes, they are generally the quietest category of rowing machine. The flywheel glides without the whoosh of air or the slosh of water, making them ideal for home gyms where noise matters.

Do I need a subscription for a magnetic rower?

Not necessarily. Many magnetic rowers work perfectly in manual or “just row” mode without any subscription. Subscription platforms like Hydrow or iFit add structured classes and scenic rows, but they are optional extras, not a requirement to use the machine.

How much maintenance does a magnetic rower require?

Very little. There are no water tanks to refill or fan blades to clean. Occasionally wipe down the rail and seat rollers, and check that the resistance mechanism stays free of dust. Most owners report years of trouble-free use with minimal upkeep.

Can tall or heavy users use magnetic rowers comfortably?

It depends on the model. Look for a long rail length (usually at least 48 inches) and a high weight capacity. Published expert reviews and owner forums are the best way to see which machines accommodate taller and heavier rowers without feeling cramped or wobbly.

What’s the difference between magnetic resistance and electromagnetic resistance?

Magnetic resistance uses a permanent magnet that you move closer to or farther from the flywheel with a knob or lever. Electromagnetic resistance uses an electromagnet controlled by the machine’s electronics, which allows automatic load changes during a workout and tighter integration with apps and pre-programmed intervals.

In shortIn the end, choosing a magnetic rower comes down to matching your budget, space, and workout style to a model with proven durability and the features you’ll actually use. Read recent owner reviews to see which machines hold up over time, and don’t be swayed by gimmicky extras you’ll never touch.
