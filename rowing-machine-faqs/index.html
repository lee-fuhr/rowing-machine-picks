Rowing machine questions answered

Updated June 2026Independently researchedNo paid placement.

Quick answer

Magnetic rowing machines use magnets to create smooth, quiet resistance that’s ideal for home use. They require very little maintenance and suit a wide range of fitness levels, but the feel differs from the rushing water of a water rower or the wind noise of an air rower. This guide answers the most common questions so you can decide if a magnetic rower is right for you.

How does a magnetic rowing machine compare to air or water rowers?

Magnetic rowers use a flywheel with magnets that create resistance without contact, so they are exceptionally quiet and smooth. Air rowers rely on a fan that whooshes louder as you pull harder, and water rowers mimic the sound and feel of rowing through water with a paddle wheel in a sealed tank. The big trade-off is that magnetic resistance doesn’t ramp up quite the same way, many users describe it as more consistent rather than progressive, though higher-end models adjust resistance in precise increments. If you live in an apartment or share a wall with a sleeping baby, a magnetic rower is the clear winner for noise. But if you want a workout that naturally intensifies with your effort and you don’t mind some sound, an air or water rower may feel more immersive. Some rowers combine magnetic and air resistance, but those are less common and typically more expensive.

Is magnetic resistance quiet enough for apartment living?

Yes, this is the main reason people choose magnetic rowers. Because the resistance mechanism never touches the flywheel, the only sounds you’ll hear are the whisper of the belt or chain and the gentle whir of the flywheel spinning. Most owners report they can row early in the morning or late at night without disturbing housemates or neighbors. That said, the rowing motion itself isn’t silent: the seat rollers on the rail make some noise, and your breathing and stroke technique add sound. But compared to an air rower that sounds like a box fan on high, a magnetic rower is practically library-grade. For the quietest setup, look for a model with a poly-V belt drive rather than a chain, and place the machine on a rubber mat to dampen vibrations.

What should I look for in a magnetic rower under a thousand dollars?

In the budget-to-midrange price band, you want a sturdy frame rated for your weight, a comfortable and wide seat, and a smooth magnetic system that offers at least eight resistance levels. Many rowers in this range use a single permanent magnet and a manual tension knob, which is reliable but doesn’t allow the same fine-tuning as an electronically controlled system. Look for a folding model if floor space is tight, some split in half for upright storage. Screen quality and connectivity vary. At this price point, you’ll often get a basic LCD showing time, distance, strokes, and calories. A few models offer Bluetooth pairing with apps like Kinomap or Zwift, but don’t expect a big color display or advanced training programs without stepping up to the next price tier. Also check the rail length: taller users (over 6 feet) need a rail of at least 48 inches for a comfortable full stroke.

Do magnetic rowing machines provide a challenging workout?

They absolutely can, but the type of challenge is different from air or water rowers. Magnetic rowers offer a set level of resistance that stays constant throughout the stroke, so you don’t get the same ‘feel’ of pulling against water that gets heavier. Instead, you control intensity by how fast and powerfully you drive, the rower doesn’t naturally push back harder. That means your workout quality depends more on your own effort. Most magnetic rowers have 8 to 16 magnetic resistance levels, and some include a fan or air damper as a secondary resistance source. For HIIT or steady-state cardio, you can definitely get your heart rate up and build endurance. But if you’re after maximum strength-building resistance, a magnetic rower may top out lower than a high-end air rower. The real advantage is the ability to dial in a precise, repeatable resistance for structured workouts or physical therapy.

How much space do I need for a magnetic rower?

A standard magnetic rower stretches about 6 to 7 feet long and 1.5 to 2 feet wide. You’ll also want at least 2 feet of clearance behind the machine for the sliding seat and a little room on each side for your elbows. If you store it upright or fold it, the footprint shrinks dramatically, many upright models take up less than 2 feet by 2 feet of floor space. Measure your storage spot before buying. Some models fold in half and stand on end, while others have a pivot mechanism that lets the rail flip up. If you plan to keep the rower assembled in a living room, consider the color and style; most are black or silver with a sleek profile. A thick exercise mat underneath protects floors and reduces noise further.

What maintenance does a magnetic rowing machine require?

Very little, which is a huge selling point. Because the resistance system has no friction pads, belts, or water to maintain, you mostly just need to keep the rail clean and the rollers free of dust and pet hair. Wipe the rail with a damp cloth after sweaty sessions, and occasionally lubricate the seat rollers or sliding bushings if the rower starts to squeak. Check the tension of the belt or chain every few months, most use a sealed bearing system that never needs lubrication. If your rower has a monitor, replace the batteries when the display gets dim. That’s about it. Compared to an air rower that may need the fan cleaned or a water rower that requires a bi-annual water purification tablet, a magnetic rower is essentially set-and-forget.

Frequently asked questions

Can magnetic rowers simulate the feel of water rowing?

Not exactly, magnetic resistance is smooth and constant, while water rowers create a dynamic load that rises and falls with your stroke. Some premium magnetic models add an air damper or an electronic variable resistance to mimic that progressive feel, but most do not. If the sensation of rowing on real water matters to you, a water rower is a better match.

Are magnetic rowers good for tall users?

Generally yes, but you need to watch the rail length and seat height. Look for a rail of at least 48 inches and an adjustable footplate that accommodates larger shoe sizes. Many mid-range magnetic rowers now offer extended rails and a higher seat profile that suit users over 6 feet 2 inches comfortably.

Do magnetic rowers require electricity?

Basic models with a manual tension knob and a battery-powered monitor do not need to be plugged in. More advanced rowers with an electronically controlled magnetic brake, backlit display, and app connectivity do require an AC adapter or batteries. Always check the specs, if you plan to place the rower far from an outlet, a self-powered model avoids cord clutter.

How long do magnetic rowing machines last?

With proper care, a well-built magnetic rower can easily last 10 to 15 years. The magnetic system itself has no wearing parts, so the main longevity concerns are the frame welds, the seat rail, and the belt or chain. Higher-end brands use steel frames and high-grade aluminum rails that hold up much better over time than cheaper steel alloys.

Is a magnetic rower good for beginners?

Yes, magnetic rowers are forgiving and allow you to set a low, consistent resistance while you learn proper form. The quiet operation also makes it easier to listen to coaching videos or music without fighting machine noise. However, beginners should still follow a structured workout plan, because the smooth resistance won’t ‘force’ you into a good rhythm like a water rower can.

Can you lift the handle on a magnetic rower?

Lifting the handle during the drive is a common form mistake, and it’s just as possible on a magnetic rower as any other type. The low handle position on most rowers encourages a straight pull, but you still have to focus on keeping your wrists flat and your hands moving in a straight line. Some magnetic rowers come with a curved handlebar that helps reinforce proper hand position.

In shortMagnetic rowing machines are a smart choice for anyone who values silence, low maintenance, and a smooth, predictable resistance curve. They’re especially well-suited to apartment dwellers, early-morning exercisers, and people rehabbing from injury. The biggest compromise is the feel, if you crave the roar and rush of water or the natural climb of an air rower, a magnetic machine might leave you wanting. But for consistent, no-nonsense cardio that won’t disturb your household, a quality magnetic rower is hard to beat.
